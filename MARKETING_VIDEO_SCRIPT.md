# Boomline — 60-Second Marketing Video Script

Director-ready storyboard for a high-energy launch / brand video aimed at crane operators, dispatchers, and owners. Designed to be cut to **Thunderstruck (AC/DC)** with timing locked to that track's structure; alternate music notes at the bottom.

The goal isn't to explain features. The goal is: **the moment a 15-year crane operator sees it, he sends it to his boss.**

---

## Concept in one line

**Paper, whiteboards, chaos → one tap → six cranes rolling.** Heavy iron. Heavy guitar. End on the platform behind it.

## Music — the licensing reality check

**Thunderstruck (AC/DC)** and **Joker and the Thief (Wolfmother)** are both fully copyrighted and ferociously expensive to license for commercial use (think $25K–$250K depending on usage rights). If this video is going on YouTube, your website, or paid ads, you need cleared rights or you'll get pulled / sued.

Three paths:

1. **License the real thing.** Go through [Lickd](https://lickd.co), [Songtradr](https://songtradr.com), or an SXM Connect rep. Expect a hard "no" or a brutal quote on Thunderstruck — it's one of the most famous riffs in rock. Wolfmother is more attainable but still pricey.
2. **Get a royalty-free track with the same energy.** Searches that work on **Artlist / Epidemic Sound / Musicbed**: *industrial rock*, *heavy guitar driving*, *blue-collar anthem*, *AC/DC style*. Tracks that consistently land in that zone:
   - "Hammer Down" — Position Music
   - "Steel & Sweat" — Artlist
   - Anything by **Audiomachine** or **Position Music** in their "industrial rock" libraries
3. **Commission an original.** $1.5K–4K via [Soundstripe Custom](https://soundstripe.com) or a freelance composer on Soundbetter. You own it forever. Best long-term move if you'll cut multiple videos.

**Recommendation**: launch with a royalty-free track that matches Thunderstruck's BPM (~134) and energy. The beat structure above transfers cleanly because crane montages care about rhythm, not the specific song.

## Voiceover

**Don't.** A voiceover on a 60-second high-energy cut feels like an explainer. This is an anthem. Let the riff and the visuals do it.

If a stakeholder insists on VO, keep it to **three lines, deep masculine working-class delivery**, dropped in the quiet moments before the drop:

> *"Cranes don't move on paper."*
> *"They move on tap."*
> (at 0:56) *"Built with trusted field experts. For crane companies."*
